1. 
 In the basic example, the external fragmentation comes out to about 24.41% (and around 27.08% in your own run). What that really means is that roughly
 a quarter of the free memory isn’t usable for large requests. The memory is technically free, but it’s split into smaller, scattered chunks instead of one
 big continuous block. This happens because processes are constantly being allocated and removed. Every time one leaves, it creates a gap, and over time
 those gaps get broken up more and more. Eventually, you end up with a lot of total free memory, but not in a form that’s actually useful.

2.
 P4 ends up using the block left by P1 simply because First-Fit grabs the first available space that’s big enough. It doesn’t try to find the best match,
 it just takes the first thing that works to save time. If that block is larger than what P4 needs, the allocator splits it. One part goes to P4, and the
 leftover becomes a new, smaller free block. That leftover space is still usable, but it can contribute to fragmentation later if it’s too small for future
 requests.

3.
The workload with many small processes (workload1.txt) creates much more fragmentation. That’s because lots of small allocations and deallocations leave
 behind tiny gaps all over memory. For example, if a 50 KB process finishes and a 45 KB process takes its spot, you're left with a microscopic 5 KB hole.
 Unless a future process needs exactly 5 KB, that space is basically dead. It’s like constantly poking tiny holes in the memory. With fewer large processes
 (workload2.txt), memory tends to be allocated and freed in massive chunks. Even if a 400 KB process takes over a 500 KB hole (leaving a 100 KB gap), the
 math works in our favor. When other massive processes nearby finish and release their space, that 100 KB gap can easily merge with them to create huge,
 reusable blocks. Moving in bigger chunks keeps things more contiguous and heavily reduces fragmentation overall.

4. 
You can have a situation where an allocation fails even though there’s enough total free memory. For example, if you have 400 KB free but it’s split into
 two separate 200 KB blocks, a request for 300 KB will fail. The reason is that memory has to be contiguous. Since neither block alone is big enough, the
 system can’t satisfy the request. This is exactly what external fragmentation looks like in practice, enough memory exists, but it’s not arranged in a
 usable way.

5.
 Best-Fit works differently by scanning all available blocks and choosing the one that most closely matches the requested size. This can sometimes help
 preserve larger blocks for later use. However, it often creates very tiny leftover fragments because it’s always trying to squeeze allocations into the
 tightest spaces. These tiny pieces are usually too small to be useful, so fragmentation still builds up, just in a different form. On top of that,
 Best-Fit is slower than First-Fit since it has to search the entire list every time.
